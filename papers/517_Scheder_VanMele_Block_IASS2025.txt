### **1. Context and Problem Statement**

The global construction industry is pressured to drastically reduce its carbon footprint while meeting urbanization demands, particularly in emerging economies. Conventional multi-story reinforced-concrete floor slabs are a major source of embodied carbon due to their inefficient structural reliance on bending to transfer loads.

* **The Alternative:** Funicular, unreinforced concrete shells offer a sustainable solution. By leveraging "strength through geometry," they act primarily in compression, significantly reducing concrete volume and completely eliminating the need for embedded steel reinforcement.


* **The Bottleneck:** The practical adoption of these complex, double-curved geometries is bottlenecked by the formwork. Traditional custom formwork is labor-intensive, costly, highly wasteful, or heavily reliant on advanced digital fabrication tools that are inaccessible in high-demand regions.



### **2. The "Unfold Form" Solution & Baseline Prototype**

To solve the formwork bottleneck, the authors propose "Unfold Form," a geometrically controlled, reusable system based on curved-crease unfolding (CCU).

* 
**System Mechanics:** Compact, flat-packed, bending-active plywood plates are joined by curved textile hinges. Through active bending, these flat assemblies manually unfold fan-like into a self-supporting, corrugated, double-curved formwork that dictates the funicular geometry of the concrete. The corrugations act as geometric stiffeners during the concrete pour and imprint structural ribs into the final cured shell.


* **Baseline Validation:** A half-scale, 2.5 m span prototype successfully validated the concept. Utilizing 8 mm plywood plates, the 24 kg formwork was deployed in under 30 minutes and successfully supported 1000 kg of wet concrete.



### **3. Parametric Methodology and Global Geometry**

The paper investigates the system's structural scalability up to architectural spans using FEA software (SOFISTIK), evaluating spans from 2.5 m to 6.0 m in 0.5 m increments. To ensure comparability, the study uses a square-plan, fan-vault topology.

* **Geometric Scaling ($\lambda$):** The system scales using a general geometric factor $\lambda$. The rise ($f$) and the curvature radius of the bending-active plates ($R$) scale proportionally ($R = \lambda \cdot R_0$) to maintain a constant rise-to-span ratio.


* **Mesh & Alignment:** Elements are modeled as quadrilateral shells (0.05 m mesh). Plywood orthotropy is intentionally aligned so the lower Young's modulus follows the curvature direction (easing bending) and the higher modulus aligns with the ruling direction (providing stiffness).



### **4. Corrugation Scaling Architectures**

A central variable in the FEA is how the corrugated ribs scale across the expanding 2.5 m to 6.0 m spans. The researchers test two extreme bounding strategies:

* 
**Strategy A (Coarse Corrugations):** The number of plates remains strictly constant at 16 per quadrant.


* 
*Effect:* As the span grows, the ribs become progressively wider (from 0.32 m to 0.76 m) and deeper.


* 
*Pros & Cons:* This significantly increases the structural depth (static height) of the formwork, aiding stability. However, deeper ribs require exponentially more wet concrete to fill, dramatically increasing the load. Visually, this creates a bold, highly expressive ribbed intrados (underside).




* 
**Strategy B (Dense Corrugations):** The rib width is locked at approximately 0.32 m.


* 
*Effect:* To fill the expanding span, the number of plates increases continuously from 16 up to 40 per quadrant.


* 
*Pros & Cons:* The concrete load remains relatively constant across scales because rib depth does not grow. However, the lack of increasing structural depth severely compromises stability, and the high part-count drastically inflates fabrication complexity and manual assembly effort. Visually, this results in a decorative, filigree aesthetic.





### **5. Mathematical Scaling Laws for Plate Thickness**

Managing the actuation force required to bend the plates on-site without causing stress failure or buckling is critical. The authors define scaling laws where $\mu$ is the plate thickness scaling factor ($t \rightarrow \mu \cdot t_0$).

* 
**Bending Moment ($M$):** $M \propto \frac{\mu^3}{\lambda}$ 


* 
**Actuation Force ($F$):** Factoring in a proportional lever arm ($l \propto \lambda$), $F \propto \frac{\mu^3}{\lambda^2}$ 


* 
**Bending Stresses ($\sigma$):** $\sigma \propto \frac{\mu}{\lambda}$ 



To dictate the behavior of $\mu$, three specific thickness scenarios were modeled:

1. **Thickness Strategy A (Constant):** $\mu = 1$. Thickness stays at 8 mm. Actuation forces drop beautifully ($F \propto 1/\lambda^2$), but the structure becomes critically flimsy.


2. **Thickness Strategy B (Proportional):** $\mu = \lambda$. Thickness scales directly with span. Stresses remain perfectly constant ($\sigma \propto 1$), but actuation forces become impractically severe for manual deployment ($F \propto \lambda$).


3. **Thickness Strategy C (Cubic Root / Target Force):** To achieve a target, manageable actuation force multiplier $\eta$, thickness is calculated as $\mu = \sqrt[3]{\lambda^2 \cdot \eta}$. By setting $\eta = 1$ (keeping deployment force exactly the same as the 2.5 m prototype), this sub-linear scaling represents the mathematical optimum between deployability and stiffness.



### **6. Load Cases, Connections, and Constraints**

* **Wet Concrete Load Cases:** The system was tested under two concreting strategies.
* 
*Load $lc$ (Constant):* An evenly distributed baseline load of $1.5~kN/m^2$ across the formwork (ignoring rib depth) to isolate geometric performance.


* *Load $lv$ (Variable):* A realistic scenario where concrete completely fills the V-shaped ribs and adds a 4 cm continuous top layer. Due to the deep ribs in Corrugation Strategy A, $lv$ results in a cubic load increase as spans approach 6 m.




* 
**Hinge Springs:** The curved hinges are modeled by releasing degrees of freedom between plates and coupling them with In-Plane (IP) and Out-Of-Plane (OOP) springs. They are tested with both constant stiffness (based on prototype textile tests) and proportionally scaled stiffness ($c = \lambda \cdot c_0$) for thicker plates.


* **Additional Supports:** Because static height is lowest near the column supports, making the formwork vulnerable to buckling, intermediate supporting battens are introduced to the perimeter frame. The FEA tests structures with zero battens, a single mid-span batten (1/2), and double battens (1/3 and 2/3 span).



### **7. Detailed FEA Structural Results**

The paper tested 10 Sample Groups (SG) to isolate variables.

* 
**Buckling and Stability:** Buckling Load Factors (BLF, denoted as $\omega$) drop roughly cubically with span. Failure consistently initiates near the outer supports where the corrugation is shallowest.


* *SG1 (Constant thickness, Coarse):* Fails rapidly. BLF drops to a highly unsafe 1.1 at 4 m spans.


* *SG8 (Proportional thickness, Dense):* Fails for spans over 3.5 m. The lack of structural depth in dense corrugations renders the system non-viable without massive external support.


* *SG5 (Cubic Root thickness + Single Support + Coarse):* The most robust realistic configuration. Achieves a highly stable BLF of $\omega = 4.11$ at the maximum 6 m span.




* 
**Deformations:** Maximum deformation ($u$) must remain below the strict threshold of $u_{crit} = 1/500$ to ensure the poured shell achieves its required funicular geometry.


* SG5 successfully stays below this limit, maxing out at an acceptable 12 mm of deflection at a 6 m span.


* Adding stiffer hinges (SG6) improves both buckling and deformation performance by one-third, resulting in the most uniform stress distribution.




* 
**Hinge Forces:** Hinge analysis shows they act primarily in tension (IP) and transverse shear (OOP). Magnitudes increase quadratically with span but remain safely below structural capacity, with minute joint displacements (under 0.5 mm), well within concrete casting tolerances.



### **8. Logistical Reflections on Materialization**

Upscaling brings practical hurdles.

1. **Fabrication:** Standard plywood sheet sizes will necessitate internal joints within individual plates. Fortunately, CCU is highly constrained by adjacent elements, making local stiffness variations from joints less structurally critical.


2. 
**Transport & Deployment:** The 6 m module is highly transportable, folding down to elements shorter than 4 m. Thicker plates (Strategy C) will require alternative hinge mechanisms (e.g., beyond basic sewn textiles), and larger spans will require greater manual labor or small machinery to overcome actuation limits.



### **9. Conclusion**

The Unfold Form system proves fully scalable up to spans of at least 6 m with no critical geometric or structural limitations. The optimal scalable configuration dictates using **coarse corrugations** (for vital static height), **cubic-root plate thickness scaling** (to balance stiffness and actuation), **stiffer scaled hinge connections**, and the inclusion of a **single intermediate support batten** to protect the vulnerable shallow edges. While dense corrugations (Strategy B) are mathematically possible if braced by multiple intermediate supports, their fabrication complexity makes them a less robust, albeit highly aesthetic, alternative.
