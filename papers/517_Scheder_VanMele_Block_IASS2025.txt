### **1. Overview and Scope**

The paper evaluates the scalability of the "Unfold Form" system, a reusable formwork for casting unreinforced, corrugated concrete shell floors. The formwork uses curved-crease unfolding (CCU) of bending-active plywood plates joined by curved textile hinges. The study relies on a finite element analysis (FEA) performed using the SOFiSTiK software (third-order theory, full geometric nonlinearity, and eigenvalue analysis for buckling) to evaluate structural stability for spans ranging from 2.5 m to 6.0 m.

### **2. Global Geometry and Boundaries**

To ensure structural symmetry, the study utilizes square-plan modules.

* 
**Span Scale:** Tested from 2.5 m to 6.0 m in 0.5 m increments.


* 
**Geometric Proportions:** The rise $f$ scales proportionally with the general geometric scaling factor $\lambda$, maintaining a constant rise-to-span ratio $f/s$. The curvature radius of the plates also scales proportionally: $R = \lambda \cdot R_0$.


* 
**Boundary Conditions:** The formwork quadrant edges are supported by a frame of perimeter planks and an internal zigzag-shaped cross. In the FE model, interfaces to the columns use pins and springs. The frame is roller-supported at its center, intersections with the cross, and the four corners of the side planks.


* 
**Intermediate Supports:** Additional supporting battens were tested in specific configurations to counteract deflection/buckling: no batten, a single batten at half-span, or double battens placed at one-third and two-thirds of the span.



### **3. Scaling Strategies**

#### **3.1 Corrugation Density**

Two corrugation configurations form the bounds of the parametric study:

* 
**Strategy A (Coarse):** The total number of plates is fixed at 16 per quadrant. As the span increases, the rib width physically grows (from 0.32 m at the 2.5 m span up to 0.76 m at the 6.0 m span).


* 
**Strategy B (Dense):** The corrugation width remains constant at approximately 0.32 m at the center. The number of plates increases with scale in discrete increments of four per quadrant (from 16 plates at 2.5 m up to 40 plates at 6.0 m).



#### **3.2 Plate Thickness and Actuation Physics**

The model utilizes quadrilateral shell elements with a 0.05 m mesh size. Plywood plates are orthotropically aligned (lower Young's modulus aligned with curvature direction). Base frame thickness is 20 mm.

The mechanical properties are governed by the geometric scale $\lambda$ and a thickness scale $\mu$ (where $t \rightarrow \mu \cdot t_0$). The study defines the physical dependencies using the following baseline equations:

* **Bending Moment:**


$$M \propto \frac{E t^3}{R} \Rightarrow M \propto \frac{\mu^3}{\lambda}$$





* **Actuation Force** (where lever arm $l \propto \lambda$):


$$F \propto \frac{M}{\lambda \cdot l} \Rightarrow F \propto \frac{\mu^3}{\lambda^2}$$





* **Target Force:** To increase the actuation force by a target factor $\eta$ ($F = F_0 \cdot \eta$):


$$\eta = \frac{\mu^3}{\lambda^2} \Rightarrow \mu = \sqrt[3]{\lambda^2 \cdot \eta}$$





* **Bending Stresses:**


$$\sigma \propto \frac{E \cdot t}{R} \Rightarrow \sigma \propto \frac{\mu}{\lambda}$$






**Thickness Scaling Scenarios Tested:**

* 
**Strategy A:** Constant thickness ($\mu = 1$).


* 
**Strategy B:** Proportional thickness ($\mu = \lambda$).


* 
**Strategy C:** Target actuation force ($\mu = \sqrt[3]{\lambda^2}$, assuming $\eta = 1$).



#### **3.3 Hinge and Concrete Load Parameters**

* 
**Hinge Stiffness:** Curved hinges are modeled by releasing degrees of freedom between plates and coupling them via in-plane and out-of-plane springs. Evaluated as **Strategy A** (constant stiffness $c$) and **Strategy B** (proportional stiffness $c = \lambda \cdot c_0$).


* 
**Load Cases:** All loads incorporate self-weight and the initial bending-active stress state. Two wet concrete loading strategies were analyzed:


* 
**Load lc (Constant):** Uniform 1.5 kN/m² area load (representing a 6.25 cm concrete layer at 24 kN/m³).


* 
**Load lv (Variable):** Representative of a filled rib with a 4 cm continuous top layer, scaling significantly in mass for coarse corrugations.





### **4. FEA Testing Matrix (Sample Groups)**

To replicate the parametric data, the authors analyzed 10 distinct configurations (SG1 to SG10) across spans from 2.5 m to 6.0 m. All tests applied both Load lc and Load lv.

* 
**SG1:** Coarse, no support, constant thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG2:** Coarse, no support, proportional thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG3:** Coarse, single support, constant thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG4:** Coarse, single support, proportional thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG5:** Coarse, single support, cubic root thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG6:** Coarse, single support, cubic root thickness, proportional stiffness.


* 
**SG7:** Coarse, double support, cubic root thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG8:** Dense, no support, proportional thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG9:** Dense, single support, cubic root thickness, constant stiffness.


* 
**SG10:** Dense, double support, cubic root thickness, constant stiffness.



### **5. Key Results for Model Validation**

If replicating the study, validate the resulting model against these documented outcomes:

* 
**Buckling & Deformation:** The buckling load factor (BLF) $\omega$ drops cubically with span size. Maximum deformation $u$ increases cubically as the span increases. The threshold for acceptable relative deformation is $u_{crit} = 1/500$.


* 
**Performance of Scenarios:** Coarse corrugation scales much better structurally than dense corrugation due to increased static depth. Pure proportional thickness (SG4) leads to safe deformations but geometrically demands unfeasible actuation forces.


* 
**Optimal Setup:** SG6 (coarse corrugations, cubic root plate thickness, single mid-span support, and proportional connection stiffness) provides the best balance of robust BLF, limited deformation, and manageable deployment forces up to a 6.0 m span. Dense corrugations require at least double intermediate supports (SG10) to avoid failure.


* 
**Hinge Displacements:** Maximum in-plane hinge displacements remain $\leq$ 0.5 mm at the 6.0 m scale, easily within acceptable concrete formwork tolerances.
